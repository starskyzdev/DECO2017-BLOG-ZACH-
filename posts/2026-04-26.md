Every design project begins with a brief. Usually we  treat it like a checklist, tick the requirements, meet the minimums, submit. But the A2 brief opens with a line that reframes the whole exercise:

> *"The brief is not a checklist. It is a conversation."*

So here is our attempt to have that conversation and to show how our discovery research shaped how we answered it.

## What BlaBla Actually Wants

BlaBla Corp builds community hubs: purpose-built spaces where people with a shared interest can connect, share, and keep coming back. 

The brief lists real, non-negotiable technical requirements; AA accessibility, responsive design, a MojoJS/SQLite/HTMX stack, sub-3-second load times, git version control throughout.

But underneath the checklist, four lines tell us what success actually looks like:

- **"Information and experience are the core of both our business and our blabber's experience."** A database full of content means nothing if the experience is flat. Both things matter equally.

- **"Curating the right experience is what keeps them coming back."** Retention is a design goal, not just an outcome. A static, browse-and-leave page is not enough.

- **"Tailor made so that groups can communicate what's important to them."** Generic will not impress. They want to see a design built for a *specific* community; not a template with different colours.

- **"A more valuable market section in which we can sell advertising space."** Engaged, returning users are worth more than one-time visitors. Our design choices need to reflect that.

## Our Community: Sourdough Bakers

We chose the sourdough baking community, specifically, serious hobby bakers who treat baking like a craft and, in many cases, like an experiment. Through our discovery research, one pattern emerged clearly: these bakers do not just want recipes. They want to understand *why* a loaf turned out the way it did, and how to do better next time.

What they actually care about is process control ;
 - starter health 
 - hydration ratios
 - fermentation
 - temperature
 - the ability to log and compare bakes over time
 
 The biggest frustration we found was not a lack of interest or resources. It was fragmentation. Most serious bakers are already juggling a hydration calculator, a starter reminder app, a notes document, and a Reddit thread, none of which talk to each other.

The insight that shaped our direction most came from community discussions: feedback becomes useful only when it comes with context. A crumb photo and a vague question gets vague answers. A structured bake log;  formula, timing, temperature, outcome, gets real help.

## What We Are Building

That gap between personal bake tracking and meaningful community feedback is our design opportunity. Our prototype is centred on a **community-driven baking lab**: a hub where bakers can log a bake in real time, capture photos and notes, review the outcome, and share the full record for structured critique and not just post into a general feed.

This is what makes our hub worth choosing over Reddit or Facebook. It is built around how sourdough bakers already think and work, not just around how social media platforms behave.
